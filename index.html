üìñ The Story Behind the Numbers

The question that started this research was simple: Does GenAI help or hurt student critical thinking? The answer, as the simulations show, is "it depends."

I discovered that GenAI is neither inherently good nor bad for learning. Instead, it acts as an amplifier of the student's existing mindset. Students with high self-confidence use GenAI to free up cognitive resources for deeper thinking. Students with low self-confidence use GenAI as a crutch, avoiding the hard work of critical thinking altogether.

The same technology produces both the best outcomes (75.3 points) and the worst outcomes (35.2 points) depending on one variable: student self-confidence.

üî¨ The Simulation

I created an agent-based model that simulates 100 students in each scenario over 100 time steps of learning activity. Each student has two key attributes:

  • Self-Confidence (0-100%): Belief in their own problem-solving abilities
  • GenAI-Confidence (0-100%): Reliance on AI tools for assistance

The model tracks how students allocate their cognitive resources between mechanical tasks (repetitive work) and critical thinking (deep analysis, evaluation, synthesis).

üßÆ The Formula

Critical thinking scores are calculated using an interaction model that captures the synergy (or dependency) between self-confidence and GenAI use:

Critical Thinking Score Formula

Base Score = 30 + (Self-Confidence √ó 50)
GenAI Effect = GenAI-Conf √ó (Self-Conf - 0.5) √ó 30
Final Score = Base Score + GenAI Effect

This formula creates the interaction effect: GenAI helps when self-confidence is high (>0.5) and hurts when self-confidence is low (<0.5).

How I determined the parameters (30 and 50): These values were calibrated to produce realistic critical thinking score distributions. The 30 sets a minimum baseline (even students with zero self-confidence start at 30 points), while the 50 creates a range where self-confidence alone can bring scores from 30 to 80. Together, these ensure that:

  • Traditional classroom students (avg self-confidence ~0.54) score around 55-60 (realistic baseline)
  • High-confidence students with GenAI score 75-80+ (excellent performance)
  • Low-confidence students with GenAI drop to 35-40 (showing the danger of dependency)
  • The scenarios produce meaningful differentiation rather than clustering together

üéØ Why This Model?

The model is based on research from Microsoft (Lee, 2025) which found:

  • "Higher self-confidence is associated with MORE critical thinking"
  • "Higher GenAI-confidence is associated with LESS critical thinking"

But the key insight is: these two effects interact. A student with high self-confidence who also uses GenAI doesn't just add the effects‚Äîthey multiply them. This creates the "synergy" we see in the best-performing scenario.

üìà What the Scores Mean

Critical Thinking Scores (0-100):

  • 60-75: Good critical thinking, effective learning

üí° How to Interpret the Data

The key finding is NOT that GenAI is good or bad. It's that GenAI is a tool that amplifies the student's approach to learning.

A confident student thinks: "I'll use GenAI to check my work and explore alternatives to MY solution." This frees up time from mechanical verification work, allowing more time for creative problem-solving.

A dependent student thinks: "I'll have GenAI do this for me because I don't think I can." This prevents the cognitive engagement necessary for skill development.

The policy implication is clear: Don't ban GenAI. Instead, focus on building student self-confidence and teaching intentional, reflective use of AI tools. The 32.2% improvement we see in the best scenario is too valuable to ignore.
